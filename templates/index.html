Direct Determinants
via Cofactor Expansion

Laplace's expansion method — computing $\det(A)$ for an $n \times n$ matrix by recursively expanding along a row using signed minors.

What is a Determinant?

The determinant of a square matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, written $\det(A)$ or $|A|$, is a scalar that encodes fundamental geometric and algebraic properties of the linear transformation represented by $A$. Geometrically, $|\det(A)|$ equals the volume scaling factor of the transformation; its sign indicates orientation preservation (+) or reversal (−).

A matrix is invertible (non-singular) if and only if $\det(A) \neq 0$. When $\det(A) = 0$, the system $Ax = b$ either has no solution or infinitely many.

Minors and Cofactors

Let $A = [a_{ij}]$ be an $n \times n$ matrix.

The $(i,j)$-minor $M_{ij}$ is the determinant of the $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ sub-matrix formed by deleting row $i$ and column $j$:

$$M_{ij} = \det\!\bigl(A \text{ with row } i \text{ and column } j \text{ removed}\bigr)$$

The $(i,j)$-cofactor attaches the appropriate sign:

$$C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} \, M_{ij}$$

The sign factor $(-1)^{i+j}$ follows the checkerboard pattern: $\begin{pmatrix}+&-&+&\cdots\\-&+&-&\cdots\\+&-&+&\cdots\end{pmatrix}$

Laplace Expansion Theorem

Theorem (Laplace, 1772): The determinant of $A$ can be computed by expanding along any row $i$:

$$\det(A) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij}\,C_{ij} = \sum_{j=1}^{n} (-1)^{i+j}\,a_{ij}\,M_{ij}$$

Or equivalently along any column $j$:

$$\det(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_{ij}\,C_{ij}$$

Both give the same result. Prefer the row or column with the most zeros to minimize arithmetic.

Base Cases and Recursion

$$\det([a]) = a \qquad (1{\times}1)$$ $$\det\!\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} = ad - bc \qquad (2{\times}2)$$

For an $n\times n$ matrix, expansion along row 1 produces $n$ cofactors, each requiring the determinant of an $(n{-}1)\times(n{-}1)$ matrix. The total multiplications grow as $O(n!)$, making this practical for $n \leq 6$ by hand. For larger systems, LU decomposition at $O(n^3)$ is preferred.

Example 1: 3×3 Matrix

Compute $\det(A)$ where $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & -2 \\ 1 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$

1

Write the Laplace expansion along Row 1.

$$\det(A) = a_{11}C_{11} + a_{12}C_{12} + a_{13}C_{13}$$ $$= a_{11}M_{11} - a_{12}M_{12} + a_{13}M_{13}$$

where $a_{11}=2,\; a_{12}=-1,\; a_{13}=3$.

2

Compute $M_{11}$ — delete row 1, column 1:

$$M_{11} = \det\!\begin{pmatrix}4 & -2 \\ 5 & 6\end{pmatrix} = (4)(6) - (-2)(5) = 24 + 10 = 34$$
3

Compute $M_{12}$ — delete row 1, column 2:

$$M_{12} = \det\!\begin{pmatrix}0 & -2 \\ 1 & 6\end{pmatrix} = (0)(6) - (-2)(1) = 0 + 2 = 2$$
4

Compute $M_{13}$ — delete row 1, column 3:

$$M_{13} = \det\!\begin{pmatrix}0 & 4 \\ 1 & 5\end{pmatrix} = (0)(5) - (4)(1) = 0 - 4 = -4$$
5

Assemble the result.

$$\det(A) = (2)(34) - (-1)(2) + (3)(-4)$$ $$= 68 + 2 - 12 = \mathbf{58}$$
det(A) = 58
6

Interpretation. Since $\det(A) = 58 \neq 0$, matrix $A$ is invertible. The linear system $Ax=b$ has a unique solution for any $b$.

Example 2: 4×4 Matrix

Compute $\det(B)$ where $B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & -1 \\ 3 & -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 & 2 \\ -2 & 0 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$

1

Expand along Row 1. Since $b_{13}=0$, that term vanishes.

$$\det(B) = b_{11}C_{11} + b_{12}C_{12} + \underbrace{b_{13}C_{13}}_{=\,0} + b_{14}C_{14}$$ $$C_{14} = (-1)^{1+4}M_{14} = -M_{14}, \quad b_{14}C_{14} = (-1)(-M_{14}) = +M_{14}$$ $$\therefore\; \det(B) = M_{11} - 2M_{12} + M_{14}$$
2

Compute $M_{11}$ — delete row 1, col 1:

$$M_{11} = \det\!\begin{pmatrix}-1&2&0\\1&4&2\\0&1&3\end{pmatrix}$$ $$= (-1)(12-2) - (2)(3-0) + 0 = -10 - 6 = -16$$
3

Compute $M_{12}$ — delete row 1, col 2:

$$M_{12} = \det\!\begin{pmatrix}3&2&0\\0&4&2\\-2&1&3\end{pmatrix}$$ $$= (3)(12-2) - (2)(0+4) + 0 = 30 - 8 = 22$$
4

Compute $M_{14}$ — delete row 1, col 4:

$$M_{14} = \det\!\begin{pmatrix}3&-1&2\\0&1&4\\-2&0&1\end{pmatrix}$$ $$= (3)(1-0) + (1)(0+8) + (2)(0+2) = 3 + 8 + 4 = 15$$
5

Assemble the result.

$$\det(B) = (-16) - 2(22) + (15) = -16 - 44 + 15 = \mathbf{-45}$$
det(B) = −45
6

Interpretation. $\det(B) = -45 \neq 0$, so $B$ is invertible. The negative sign indicates the transformation reverses orientation in $\mathbb{R}^4$.
